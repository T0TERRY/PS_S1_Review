Chapter 1: Self-Awareness ü™û +

Quick Summary

Self-awareness means knowing yourself - your personality, emotions, behaviors, and how you affect others. It's the foundation of good people skills.

1. Self-Awareness
Understanding your own personality, emotions, desires, and reasons for acting. Knowing how your behavior affects yourself and others.
üìñ Example:
A teacher realizes they become impatient when the classroom is noisy, so they practice calming down before responding to students.
2. Think-Feel-Do Framework
A simple way to understand learning and working with people by dividing it into three connected parts:
Think (Cognitive) - Being aware of what you're doing and thinking carefully, not just following routines
Feel (Emotional) - Recognizing that your feelings affect how you learn and work
Do (Behavioral) - Taking action and practicing skills, applying knowledge
üìñ Example:
Student giving a presentation: Think - Plans what to say clearly. Feel - Notices feeling nervous. Do - Practices in front of a mirror to build confidence.
3. Unconditional Positive Regard
Accepting and respecting others without judging them, even when they make mistakes.
üìñ Example:
A coach supports a player after a poor performance and focuses on improvement rather than blame.
4. Triggers
Events or situations that cause strong emotional reactions, often connected to past experiences.
üìñ Example:
Someone feels angry when criticized because of negative experiences in the past. After recognizing this trigger, they work on responding calmly.
5. Blind Spots
Behaviors or traits we don't notice in ourselves, but others can see clearly.
üìñ Example:
A speaker doesn't realize they interrupt others during discussions until classmates point it out.
6. Mixed Messages
When spoken words don't match body language or tone of voice, causing confusion.
üìñ Example:
A person says they're happy to help but sounds annoyed, making others unsure how they really feel.
7. Transference
When feelings from past relationships affect how we react to people in the present.
üìñ Example:
An employee feels nervous around a supervisor because they remind them of a very strict teacher from the past.
8. The Johari Window
A model that explains what we know about ourselves and what others know about us, including open, hidden, blind, and unknown areas.
üìñ Example:
After asking for feedback, a student learns that others see them as confident even though they thought they appeared shy.
Chapter 2: Time Management ‚è∞ +

Quick Summary

Time management is organizing and planning how to use your time effectively. It's a learnable skill that helps you work smarter, meet deadlines, and reduce stress.

1. Effectiveness
Achieving your goals and focusing on what matters most.
üìñ Example:
A student studies the most important topics for an exam rather than minor details, getting a higher score.
2. Efficiency
Completing tasks using the least time and resources, doing things quickly and easily.
üìñ Example:
Using keyboard shortcuts to format a document faster, finishing it in half the usual time.
3. The Three-Minute Rule
If a task takes three minutes or less, do it immediately instead of postponing it.
üìñ Example:
Replying to a short email right away instead of leaving it in the inbox.
4. The Worst-First Rule
Do the hardest or least pleasant tasks first to avoid procrastination.
üìñ Example:
A student finishes their most difficult assignment in the morning, feeling less stressed the rest of the day.
5. Time and Energy
Managing time also means managing energy, as some tasks require more focus and effort than others.
üìñ Example:
An employee does creative tasks in the morning when energy is highest and routine tasks in the afternoon.
6. Work/Rest Ratio
Balancing work and rest prevents burnout. Common method: 50 minutes work + 10 minutes rest.
üìñ Example:
A writer works for 45 minutes, then takes a 15-minute break to recharge before continuing.
7. The Expanding Workload (Parkinson's Law)
Work tends to expand to fill the available time, which can reduce efficiency.
üìñ Example:
A report due in three hours may take the full time even though it could be finished in two hours.
8. Timing
People have different energy peaks; knowing your best times helps plan tasks more effectively.
üìñ Example:
A student schedules difficult study topics for the afternoon when they feel most alert, and easier tasks for the morning.
9. The Best Person for the Job
Assigning tasks to people who are good at them and enjoy them improves motivation and productivity.
üìñ Example:
In a team, a member who excels at data analysis handles the numbers, while another who enjoys writing creates the report.
Chapter 3: Stress Management üò∞ +

Quick Summary

Stress is NOT the same as pressure. Stress is a person's negative response to inappropriate pressure levels. Too much OR too little pressure causes stress, but appropriate pressure can be motivating.

üéØ Key Understanding

  • Stress = Always harmful and negative
  • Pressure = Unavoidable and can be beneficial
  • Whether stress occurs depends on both pressure level AND individual response
  • Stress is highly personal - everyone responds differently
1. Stress
A person's negative reaction to an inappropriate level of pressure.
üìñ Example:
A student feels anxious and exhausted because they have too many exams in one week.
2. Pressure
The demand or expectation placed on a person, which can be positive or negative.
üìñ Example:
A deadline motivates an employee to work efficiently.
3. Response
The way a person reacts to pressure emotionally, mentally, or physically.
üìñ Example:
One worker stays calm under pressure, while another becomes overwhelmed.
4. Coping Resources
The skills, support, and tools used to manage pressure and stress.
üìñ Example:
Good time management, supportive friends, and exercise help someone cope with stress.
5. Objective Setting
Setting clear and realistic goals to provide direction.
üìñ Example:
A teacher sets a goal to prepare one lesson per day instead of trying to do everything at once.
6. Cognitive Restructuring
Changing negative or unhelpful thoughts to reduce stress.
üìñ Example:
Thinking "I will try my best" instead of "I must be perfect."
7. Boundaries of Responsibility
Knowing what you are responsible for and what is not your duty.
üìñ Example:
An employee completes their own tasks without taking responsibility for a colleague's work.
8. Active Coping
Directly dealing with the source of stress.
üìñ Example:
Creating a study schedule to prepare for exams.
9. Passive Coping
Avoiding or escaping from the stress rather than solving it.
üìñ Example:
Ignoring assignments and spending time on entertainment to forget about stress.
10. Self-Compassion
Treating yourself with understanding rather than harsh self-criticism.
üìñ Example:
Accepting a mistake, learning from it, and moving on without blaming yourself.
Chapter 4: Information Management üìä +

Quick Summary

Information management is essential because we receive huge amounts of information daily. Without careful management, it becomes overwhelming and leads to mistakes and stress. The key is being selective and organized.

1. Information Management
The process of collecting, organizing, storing, retrieving, and disposing of information effectively.
üìñ Example:
Sorting emails into folders for action, reference, or deletion.
2. Correspondence
Written communication received or sent within or outside an organization. Two types: external (letters, emails) and internal (emails).
üìñ Example:
Emails from colleagues and letters from external agencies.
3. Routine Matters
Tasks or information that follow standard procedures and require minimal judgment.
üìñ Example:
Filing a completed attendance form.
4. Exceptional Matters
Unusual or complex situations that require special attention or individual judgment.
üìñ Example:
Handling a complaint that doesn't follow normal procedures.
5. Information Overload
A situation where the amount of information exceeds a person's ability to process it effectively.
üìñ Example:
Returning from leave to hundreds of unread emails.
6. Storage
The method of keeping information for future use, either physically or electronically.
üìñ Example:
Saving documents in labelled folders on a computer.
7. Retrieval
The ability to locate and access stored information when needed.
üìñ Example:
Finding a past report quickly using a clear filing system.
8. Confidential Information
Information that must be protected from unauthorized access.
üìñ Example:
Personal records stored in a locked cabinet or password-protected file.
9. Disposal
The safe and appropriate removal of information that is no longer needed.
üìñ Example:
Shredding confidential documents.
10. Selectivity
The skill of choosing which information to read, keep, or ignore.
üìñ Example:
Skimming newsletters and reading only relevant sections.
11. Processing Information
Organizing information into categories such as action, pending, or reference.
üìñ Example:
Adding urgent emails to a task list and saving others for reference.
Chapter 5: Assertiveness üí™ +

Quick Summary

Assertiveness is the balance between being submissive (letting others walk over you) and being aggressive (walking over others). It's about expressing yourself honestly while respecting others.

üéØ Three Behavioral Styles

  • Non-assertive (Submissive): Not expressing needs, saying yes when you mean no
  • Aggressive: Disregarding others' needs, generating resentment
  • Assertive: Expressing yourself while respecting others

üéØ Jakubowski's 8 Tenets of Assertiveness

  • Standing up for yourself builds self-respect
  • Avoiding conflict causes greater harm later
  • Direct expression helps everyone
  • Hiding feelings damages relationships
  • Openness makes relationships genuine
  • Not expressing feelings is as bad as not listening
  • Giving up rights encourages others to take advantage
  • Assertiveness creates respectful relationships
1. Assertiveness
Expressing one's thoughts, feelings, and needs honestly and respectfully while respecting others.
üìñ Example:
Calmly telling a colleague that their late reports affect your work.
2. Self-Esteem
A person's overall sense of self-worth.
üìñ Example:
Feeling confident enough to share your opinion in a meeting.
3. Vicious Circle
A negative cycle where one problem reinforces another.
üìñ Example:
Low self-esteem ‚Üí silence ‚Üí lack of respect ‚Üí lower self-esteem.
4. Direct Communication
Clearly expressing thoughts and feelings without hints or manipulation.
üìñ Example:
Saying "I feel frustrated when deadlines are missed" instead of making sarcastic comments.
5. Coercion
Trying to control others through pressure, guilt, or intimidation.
üìñ Example:
Making someone feel guilty to force them to agree.
Chapter 6: Beating the Bully üõ°Ô∏è +

Quick Summary

Workplace bullying is the misuse of power that causes distress and harm. It has serious consequences including stress, loss of confidence, health problems, and even resignation. Dealing with it requires action at individual, team, organizational, and sometimes legal levels.

üéØ Forms of Bullying

  • Intimidation
  • Humiliation
  • Excessive criticism
  • Discriminatory behavior

üéØ Action Levels

  • Individual: Assertive responses
  • Team: Support from colleagues
  • Organization: Duty of care and policies
  • Legal: Remedies when necessary
1. Whistle Blowing
Reporting wrongdoing within an organization.
üìñ Example:
Reporting bullying through a confidential reporting system.
2. Legal Remedy
Using the law to seek justice or compensation.
üìñ Example:
Taking a workplace harassment case to court.

üí° Important Tips

  • Keep records of bullying incidents
  • Seek support - don't face it alone
  • Don't accept bullying as normal
  • Know your rights and organization's policies
Chapter 7: Using Supervision üë• +

Quick Summary

Supervision is how organizations support and empower staff to achieve goals. It's not just monitoring - it's about development, support, and problem-solving. Good supervision reduces stress; poor supervision increases it.

üéØ 4 Key Elements of Supervision

  • Accountability: Ensures work meets standards, provides quality control
  • Staff Development: Learning, skill building, overcoming barriers
  • Staff Care: Emotional support, stress management, safe space
  • Mediation: Managing relationships, resolving conflicts

üí° Making the Most of Supervision

  • Avoid 'macho' attitudes: Asking for help is not weakness
  • Don't collude: Avoid unproductive behaviors
  • Ensure supervision: Be proactive in scheduling
  • Identify needs: Communicate what you need
  • Be assertive: Stand up for your rights respectfully
  • Use time management: Allocate time for supervision
  • Prepare: Set priorities and plan issues
1. Supervision
A structured process through which an organization supports, monitors, and develops staff to achieve goals and maintain quality.
2. 'Macho' Attitude
A "be tough, do it alone" mindset where refusing help is seen as strength. This blocks effective supervision because supervision requires openness to guidance and support.
üìñ Example:
A worker refuses to discuss challenges with their supervisor because they think it shows weakness.
Chapter 8: Being Creative üé® +

Quick Summary

Creativity isn't just for artists - it's a valuable skill in all work. These techniques help develop creative thinking and problem-solving abilities.

1. Changing Angle
Viewing a situation from a different perspective to gain fresh insights.
üìñ Example:
Instead of asking "Why is this customer complaining?" ask "What can we learn from this feedback?"
2. Developing a Vision
Having clear objectives to guide progress and stimulate creative solutions.
üìñ Example:
A team visualizes their ideal customer service experience to guide improvements.
3. Stepping Back
Distancing yourself from a situation to gain perspective and clarity ("see the wood for the trees").
üìñ Example:
Taking a break from a difficult project to see the big picture more clearly.
4. Letting Go
Brainstorming freely without judgment to generate creative ideas, including unusual ones that can inspire solutions.
üìñ Example:
A team suggests wild ideas like "what if we gave products away free?" to spark creative thinking about pricing strategies.
5. Provocation
Breaking habitual mental patterns (what-if thinking) to unlock new insights (concept from de Bono).
üìñ Example:
"What if we had no office?" might lead to innovative remote work solutions.
Chapter 9: Continuous Professional Development üìà +

Quick Summary

CPD is ongoing learning after formal education. It keeps you effective, up-to-date, and prevents burnout. Learning from experience requires reflection, not just doing the work.

üéØ Why CPD Matters

  • Job satisfaction: Keeps work interesting
  • Improved practice: Better skills and knowledge
  • Avoiding burnout: Fresh perspectives prevent stagnation
  • Personal development: Growth as a professional
  • Adventure of theory: Practice based on knowledge, not guesswork
  • It's dangerous not to: "Overlearning" = working mechanically without thinking

üîÑ Kolb's Learning Cycle (4 Stages)

  • 1. Concrete Experience: Day-to-day work experience
  • 2. Reflective Observation: Reflecting on what happened
  • 3. Abstract Conceptualization: Linking to previous learning, forming mini-theories
  • 4. Active Experimentation: Trying new ideas in practice
1. CPD (Continuous Professional Development)
The ongoing process of learning and improving skills, knowledge, and professional competence after formal education or initial training.
üìñ Example:
A teacher attends workshops, reads journals, and reflects on teaching methods to improve continuously.
2. Secondment
Temporary transfer to another job, role, or department to gain new skills and experience.
üìñ Example:
An HR employee temporarily works in the marketing department for 6 months to learn new skills.
Chapter 16: Influencing Skills üéØ +

Quick Summary

Influencing is about persuading others effectively through connection, transparency, solutions, and empathy - not manipulation or force.

1. Connection
Engaging and forming rapport with others to build a foundation for influence.
üìñ Example:
Starting a meeting with casual conversation to build trust before presenting ideas.
2. Putting Cards on the Table
Being open about goals and encouraging transparency to identify common interests.
üìñ Example:
"My goal is to reduce meeting time. What are your concerns about this?"
3. Giving Solutions, Not Problems
Presenting both the problem and a proposed solution to increase effectiveness.
üìñ Example:
"The reports are late (problem). I suggest we use a shared calendar with reminders (solution)."
4. Giving Feedback
Providing positive or constructive criticism tactfully to gain respect and influence.
üìñ Example:
"Your presentation was clear, and adding more visuals would make it even stronger."
5. Speaking the Same Language
Matching language, style, or terminology to the other person for better communication.
üìñ Example:
Using technical terms with IT staff but simple language with clients.
6. Dealing with Objections
Identifying and responding to resistance or concerns openly and constructively.
üìñ Example:
"I understand your concern about cost. Let me show you the long-term savings."
7. Listening and Empathy
Actively listening and understanding others' perspectives to build credibility and respect.
üìñ Example:
"So you're worried this change will increase your workload. How can we address that?"
Chapter 18: Handling Feelings üíù +

Quick Summary

Handling feelings is crucial at work but often neglected. Feelings can motivate (accelerators) or demotivate (brakes). Awareness of feelings is essential for effective and safe practice.

üéØ Key Differences

  • Sympathy: Sharing someone's feelings (if they're sad, you are too)
  • Empathy: Recognizing feelings without necessarily feeling them yourself
3. Avoid Macho Approach
Repressing emotions or seeing them as a weakness is unhealthy.
üìñ Example:
Saying "I'm fine" when clearly upset instead of expressing genuine feelings.
4. Avoid Self-Indulgent Voyeurism
Becoming overly focused on others' feelings for personal satisfaction - enjoying their stories to the point of forgetting your work.
üìñ Example:
A counselor who asks excessive personal questions out of curiosity rather than to help the client.

üéØ Responding Appropriately to Different Feelings

  • Loss: Be present; don't rush advice
  • Anger: Choose words carefully to avoid escalation
  • Guilt: Offer reassurance; allow time to process
  • Joy: Keep perspective without killing enthusiasm
  • Anxiety: Help translate general unease into specific fears for action
6. Catharsis
A strategy people use to release emotional blockages to regain control and empowerment.
üìñ Example:
Talking through a traumatic event with a counselor to process and release pent-up emotions.
7. Avoid Stoic Responses
Repressing feelings leads to wasted energy, stifled problem-solving, and burnout.
8. Ethos of Permission
Creating a culture where expressing feelings is acceptable and supported.
Express your own feelings
Accept others' expression of feelings
Offer support when colleagues are distressed
Include emotions in work discussions
9. Counter-Transference
The worker's previous experiences influencing their response to a client, leading to biased or unbalanced treatment.
üìñ Example:
A social worker being overly protective of a client because they remind them of their own child.
10. Mirroring
When the emotional dynamics of clients/colleagues are reflected in our own behavior or feelings, often unconsciously.
üìñ Example:
If a client avoids discussing emotional issues, the worker may also feel uncomfortable and start avoiding these topics.
11. Professional Accommodation Syndrome (PAS)
Workers' stress gets worse because their organization responds insensitively to the emotional pressures of the job.
Primary Pressure: Stress from the work itself (e.g., difficult clients)
Secondary Pressure: Stress from lack of organizational support (e.g., supervisors ignoring emotional issues)
üìñ Example:
A nurse dealing with patient deaths (primary pressure) receives no emotional support from management (secondary pressure), leading to burnout.
Chapter 21: Anti-Discriminatory Practice ‚öñÔ∏è +

Quick Summary

Understanding different forms of discrimination and unfair treatment helps us create more equitable workplaces and societies. These concepts explain how discrimination operates.

1. Oppression
Long-term and systematic unfair treatment of people or groups, usually based on social characteristics like race, gender, or class.
üìñ Example:
In a workplace where women are consistently paid less than men for the same role, women are experiencing oppression.
2. Marginalisation
When certain people or groups are pushed to the edges of society, limiting their access to resources, opportunities, or rights.
üìñ Example:
Homeless individuals who cannot access healthcare or education due to societal barriers are marginalized.
3. Group Closure
When a group deliberately restricts membership or participation to certain people, often excluding others based on identity.
üìñ Example:
An English school that only hires graduates from IFL practices group closure.
4. Stereotyping
Assigning broad, often inaccurate characteristics to people based on their identity or appearance.
üìñ Example:
Assuming that all elderly people are bad with technology is a form of stereotyping.
5. Stigmatisation
Labeling someone or a group negatively, which can lead to social rejection or discrimination.
üìñ Example:
People with physical disabilities being treated as "incapable" or "less competent" are stigmatized.
6. Scapegoating
Blaming an individual or group for problems that are not their fault, often to divert attention from the real causes.
üìñ Example:
Blaming a single employee for a company's declining sales, when management decisions are the real cause.
7. Resentment
Feeling bitter or angry toward someone or something seen as unfair.
üìñ Example:
A student might feel resentment toward a teacher who favors certain students.
8. Mystification
Making something confusing or complex to prevent people from understanding it, often to retain control.
üìñ Example:
A company using overly technical jargon in contracts to make it hard for customers to fully understand the terms.
9. Short-Lived Success
Achieving a goal temporarily, but not maintaining it over time.
üìñ Example:
A new restaurant that attracts crowds for a few months but quickly loses popularity experiences short-lived success.
10. Group Influence
The way people's attitudes, beliefs, or behaviors are shaped by the group they belong to or interact with.
üìñ Example:
A student who starts studying more regularly because their friends are highly disciplined is experiencing group influence.
Chapter 22: Being Systematic üìã +

Quick Summary

Being systematic means having clear, measurable, and achievable plans. It prevents drift and vagueness that waste time and resources.

1. Drift
A slow, unplanned change in direction or focus over time.
üìñ Example:
A school curriculum may drift from its original purpose if teachers gradually add content without reviewing the overall objectives.
2. Vagueness
When something is unclear, imprecise, or lacks detail.
üìñ Example:
A supervisor saying "do better next time" without specifying what to improve is being vague.
3. Measurability
The ability to evaluate or quantify something clearly using specific criteria.
üìñ Example:
The success of a fitness program can be measured by the number of participants who meet their target weight or fitness goals.
4. Achievability
How realistic it is to reach a goal, considering resources, constraints, and time.
üìñ Example:
Setting a goal to run a marathon in a week without training is not achievable, but planning for a marathon in six months is realistic.
Chapter 23: Assessment üìù +

Quick Summary

Assessment is the preparatory stage that establishes firm ground for later helping processes. It guides intervention, review, evaluation, and ending decisions.

üéØ Assessment's Role In:

  • Intervention: Acts as basis for action plan, sets objectives
  • Review and Evaluation: Becomes baseline for measuring progress
  • Ending: Helps determine when objectives are met and intervention can close
1. Powers
What workers are empowered to do in certain circumstances.
üìñ Example:
A social worker has the power to recommend family support services.
2. Duties
What workers must do in certain circumstances.
üìñ Example:
Teachers have a duty to report suspected child abuse.
3. Restrictions
Limitations on what workers are allowed to do.
üìñ Example:
A counselor cannot share client information without consent (restriction).
4. Paternalism
Managing or controlling others in a way that restricts their freedom for their own perceived good.
üìñ Example:
A company adopts paternalistic policies by making health benefit decisions for employees without consulting them.
5. Confidentiality
Keeping information private and ensuring it's not shared with unauthorized individuals.
üìñ Example:
Medical records kept secure and only shared with authorized healthcare providers.
6. Frozen Watchfulness
A state of vigilant but passive observation where individuals wait without acting, often due to uncertainty or lack of confidence.
üìñ Example:
During a crisis, employees demonstrate frozen watchfulness by hesitating to take action while awaiting instructions from higher-ups.

Chapter 26: Managing Risk ‚ö†Ô∏è +

Quick Summary

Managing risk involves finding the balance between being too cautious (paranoid) and too careless (naive). Different strategies help manage different types of risks.

1. Avoidance
Deliberately steering clear of actions that might involve risk or unwanted outcomes.
üìñ Example:
A school might avoid introducing a new software system until teachers are fully trained to prevent confusion or errors.
2. Elimination
Completely removing a risk or hazard from a situation.
üìñ Example:
A factory replacing slippery floors with anti-slip surfaces eliminates the risk of employee falls.
3. Naivety
A lack of experience or judgment that leads to overly simple or unrealistic thinking.
üìñ Example:
A new driver might show naivety by assuming they can drive safely in heavy rain without reducing speed.
4. Paranoia
An irrational feeling that others are plotting against you or threatening you, without real evidence.
üìñ Example:
An employee might feel paranoia if they think everyone is trying to get them fired, even though there's no proof.
5. The Contingency of Being
Life's unpredictability, where outcomes are often shaped by chance or unforeseen events.
üìñ Example:
The contingency of being can be seen when someone unexpectedly inherits a property that changes their life plans.

Chapter 28: Ending üèÅ +

Quick Summary

Once intervention is done, it's important to know when to stop providing help. Proper ending is essential for several reasons.

üéØ Why Practice Ending?

  • Manage workload: Frees up time and resources for new cases
  • Avoid labelling: Prevents people from becoming dependent or being labeled as "needing help"

  • Smooth transfer: Ensures proper handover to relevant stakeholders if needed

üí° Good Ending Practice

  • Plan endings from the beginning
  • Prepare clients/stakeholders for ending
  • Review achievements and learning
  • Ensure sustainability of changes
  • Provide clear referrals if needed
Chapter 29: Responding to Trauma üÜò +

Quick Summary

When dealing with trauma, three key principles guide effective and safe practice: understanding personal meaning, working together for mutual support, and not getting overwhelmed.

üéØ Three Key Principles

  • 1. Tuning in to meaning: Understand personal significance people attach to experiences; use storytelling to help them make sense
  • 2. Working together: Provide mutual support among helpers; trauma creates powerlessness that spreads
  • 3. Not getting out of our depth: Be prepared through training; don't take on more than you can handle; use teamwork
1. Retraumatisation
When talking about a trauma triggers more distress.
üìñ Example:
Asking a survivor to relive a car accident too soon can cause panic.
2. Secondary Traumatisation
When people close to or helping the trauma survivor experience trauma-like effects.
üìñ Example:
A social worker supporting abuse victims may feel anxious or exhausted over time.

üí° Best Practices

  • Listen to survivors' stories with empathy
  • Don't force people to share before they're ready
  • Provide support for helpers too
  • Get proper training before trauma work
  • Work as a team, never alone
  • Recognize signs of secondary trauma in yourself
